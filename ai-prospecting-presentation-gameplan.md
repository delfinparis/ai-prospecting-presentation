### HERO #1 — The Pre-Listing Intelligence Dossier (Claude Pro)
*Universal — works for every avatar. Opens the talk because the live demo is the most jaw-dropping.*

**Live demo plan:** Use a public figure's publicly known residence, or a historical sale (a celebrity home that sold 3+ years ago, where all details are in the press). No privacy risk, full drama.

```
ROLE:
You are a senior real estate strategist briefing a top listing agent 15 minutes before a listing appointment. You are not a marketer. You are a researcher with access to public records, news, and social media.

CONTEXT:
I have a listing appointment tomorrow at [FULL ADDRESS] with a homeowner named [FULL NAME]. I have no other information about them. I need a briefing so thorough that when I walk in, the seller feels like I've known them for a year.

TASK:
Using deep web research, build a one-page pre-listing dossier on this homeowner and this property. Pull from:
- County assessor and recorder public records (purchase date, price, tax history, loan, any liens, prior sales)
- Building permits filed on the address in the last 10 years
- LinkedIn, company websites, any press, podcast appearances, charitable involvement, board memberships
- Local news mentions of the name or the household
- Social media public posts (any platform) in the last 12 months
- Publicly listed family details: spouse, kids' approximate ages, schools (only if publicly stated)
- Neighborhood-specific market data for the last 90 days: median sale, DOM, active vs sold ratio, three most comparable recent sales within 0.5 miles

CONSTRAINTS:
- Use only publicly available sources. No scraping, no guessing paid-database content.
- Clearly flag every piece of information as either VERIFIED (with source link) or INFERRED (with reasoning).
- Do not fabricate any fact. If you cannot find something, say "not found" — do not invent.
- Keep the final dossier to one page.

RULES FOR THE DOSSIER:
1. Lead with the single most important insight about this seller's likely motivation (e.g., "recently named partner, equity position strong, likely upsizing").
2. Pricing psychology: based on public records, what anchor price is this seller mentally attached to? (Original purchase price, last refi appraisal, the Zestimate, a neighbor's recent sale.)
3. Top 3 likely objections ranked by probability, with a one-sentence pre-handled response for each.
4. Two rapport-building hooks that are not real estate — things I can genuinely reference from public info (a cause they support, a company win, a kid's sports team, a hobby).
5. One non-obvious insight about this specific micro-market that a competing agent would not know.
6. One question I should ask in the first 10 minutes that will reveal the real timeline.

EXPECTED OUTPUT:
A one-page brief, executive tone, in this exact structure:
- HEADLINE INSIGHT (2 sentences)
- SELLER SNAPSHOT (bulleted, with VERIFIED/INFERRED tags)
- PROPERTY & MARKET (bulleted)
- PRICING ANCHOR (1 paragraph)
- TOP 3 OBJECTIONS + PRE-HANDLES (table)
- 2 RAPPORT HOOKS (bulleted)
- THE ONE QUESTION (bolded)
- SOURCES (linked list)
```

**Why this lands:** In a live demo, paste in a public figure's address. The audience watches a researcher-grade dossier generate in ~90 seconds. Nobody has seen this run live on a real property. Tom Ferry and Jimmy Burgess both cite dossier prompts but in a much weaker form — this one has the VERIFIED/INFERRED tagging and the pricing-psychology layer, which is the differentiator.

### HERO #2 — The Database Triage ("Who do I call Monday at 9am?") (Claude Pro)
*Warm. For every avatar with a CSV, which by the end of the talk should be every avatar.*

```
ROLE:
You are a top-producing real estate coach who specializes in database reactivation. You have seen thousands of agent CRMs. Your job is to tell an agent which 10 people to call this week — and why — based entirely on their database.

CONTEXT:
Attached is my CRM export as a CSV. Columns: name, phone, email, last_contact_date, source, tags, freeform_notes, city, year_purchased, purchase_price, estimated_current_value, birthday, life_event_notes. There are [N] contacts. I have 60 minutes of call time this week and I need to spend it on the 10 highest-probability conversations.

TASK:
Score every contact 1–100 on probability of transacting a real estate deal (buy, sell, or refer) in the next 6 months. Then return the top 10 as a prioritized call list.

SCORING WEIGHTS (apply all, then combine):
1. Years in home: 5–9 years = peak sell probability. Under 2 or over 15 = lower. Weight: 25%.
2. Equity position: estimated_current_value minus purchase_price, as a percent. >40% equity = high. Weight: 20%.
3. Life-event signals in notes: baby, divorce, marriage, promotion, retire, empty nest, relocation, new job, illness, inheritance, aging parent. Any hit = major boost. Weight: 25%.
4. Recency decay: not touched in 120+ days = penalty, UNLESS a life-event signal overrides. Weight: 10%.
5. Past referral behavior: anyone who's ever sent a referral gets a multiplier. Weight: 10%.
6. Freeform notes richness: contacts with rich personal notes are easier to open a real conversation with. Weight: 10%.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Do not invent life events that are not in the notes.
- Do not confuse "hot lead" with "good call target." A person with low equity but a life event is still a great call.
- If a contact has no notes at all, they are not eligible for the top 10.
- Penalize anyone flagged "do not contact" or "DNC" in any column.

RULES FOR THE OUTPUT:
For each of the top 10, return:
- Rank
- Name
- Score /100
- The single strongest signal (one phrase)
- The specific note that triggered it, quoted
- A suggested opening line (one sentence, conversational, references the note, no "just checking in," no "the market is crazy right now")

Also return:
- A "nurture only, do not call this week" list of the next 20, with one-line reasons.
- A "dead weight" count of contacts that need to be scrubbed from the CRM entirely.

EXPECTED OUTPUT:
Three tables, no preamble, no summary paragraph. Just the tables.
```

**Why this lands:** The part-timer and struggling full-timer both have exported a CSV and stared at it. Nobody has ever told them who to call. The live demo — paste in a sample CSV, watch it rank — produces an audible reaction. The "quoted note + opening line" combo is what makes it feel like a coach, not a spreadsheet.

**Follow-up power prompt to tease on stage:** *"Now re-run this assuming mortgage rates drop 75 bps next quarter. Who moves up the list?"*

### HERO #3 — The Ghost Notes Reactivation (Claude Pro)
*Warm. Built for Marcus and Renee — the rookie and the burned-out agent — but every avatar has ghost notes.*

```
ROLE:
You are a real estate re-engagement strategist. You specialize in finding gold in CRM notes that the original agent forgot they wrote. You are empathetic, specific, and allergic to generic outreach.

CONTEXT:
Below are raw CRM notes from leads I have not contacted in 18+ months. These notes were written by past-me in a hurry — some are one line, some are paragraphs. Most of these people I've written off. I believe at least 20% of them are still in the market or know someone who is, and I want to re-engage them in a way that feels like an old friend reaching out, not a desperate agent rebooting a dead database.

NOTES DUMP:
[PASTE ALL NOTES HERE — as messy as they are, one lead per block, name first]

TASK:
For every lead in the dump, do three things:

1. RECONSTRUCT: In two sentences, summarize what I knew about this person at the time — their situation, what they wanted, what was blocking them. Include the specific details that make them a human, not a row (kids' names, jobs, neighborhoods, hobbies).

2. AGE-FORWARD: Assume 18–36 months have passed since the note. Based on life-stage logic, what has probably changed for this person? (The pregnant wife now has a toddler. The guy looking in a $600K range may now be priced out or, if he waited, priced in. The couple that was "waiting to get married" is likely married.) Be specific and reasonable, not speculative.

3. REACTIVATE: Write a four-touch reactivation sequence tailored to this specific person:
   - Touch 1: A 2-sentence text. References one specific detail from the notes. Does not mention real estate. Tone: an old friend who suddenly remembered them.
   - Touch 2 (sent 3 days later if no reply): A 90-word email. Still doesn't pitch. Offers one genuinely useful piece of information tied to their original situation.
   - Touch 3 (sent 7 days later): A 20-second voice memo script. First-name opening. One question. Ends with "no rush, just thinking about you."
   - Touch 4 (sent 14 days later): A handwritten card message, 4 sentences max. References a specific thing. Does not mention real estate at all.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Never use the phrase "just checking in."
- Never apologize for the time gap. Acknowledge it naturally if it fits ("been a minute") but never grovel.
- Never use "crazy market" or "interest rates" as a hook.
- Match the tone of the original notes — if I wrote the notes in a casual voice, keep it casual. If the lead was formal, stay formal.
- If the original note is too thin to personalize (under 10 meaningful words), output: "NOT ENOUGH TO PERSONALIZE — skip or scrub" for that lead. Do not fake a sequence.

RULES FOR THE OUTPUT:
- Organize by lead. For each: RECONSTRUCT paragraph, AGE-FORWARD paragraph, then the four touches clearly labeled.
- Before the full output, give me a summary table: lead name, confidence this lead is re-engageable (High/Medium/Low), and the single strongest hook you're using.

EXPECTED OUTPUT:
Summary table first, then per-lead sequences, in the exact order the leads appeared in the input.
```

**Why this lands:** Every agent in the room has an old CRM or a pile of business cards. This query turns dead weight into appointments. The no-apologize, no-just-checking-in, no-crazy-market rule set is what makes the output sound human — and is the single biggest differentiator from what the average agent is already getting out of ChatGPT.

### HERO #4 — Business Entity Unmasking: Finding the Human Behind the Investor (Claude Pro)
*Cold. The jaw-drop moment. Non-vulnerable population, uses only public Secretary of State records, produces a concrete human name in under five minutes. Works on LLCs, corporations (Inc), LPs, and any other registered business entity.*

```
ROLE:
You are a real estate intelligence analyst who specializes in tracing entity-owned residential and small commercial properties back to the human decision-makers behind them. You use only publicly available sources — county records, Secretary of State filings, LinkedIn, business press. Your job is to give a real estate agent a real name and a real way to reach them.

CONTEXT:
In my farm area — [CITY/NEIGHBORHOOD], [STATE] — a growing percentage of properties are owned by business entities (LLCs, corporations, LPs, trusts). Tax records show the entity name but not the human behind it. I want to identify the actual people so I can build relationships with them as potential sellers, repeat buyers, and referral sources. These are investors and business owners, not distressed homeowners — they are sophisticated, they welcome good agents, and they are a durable source of repeat business.

INPUT:
[Paste a list of 5–25 entity names and the property addresses they own in my farm. Columns: Entity Name | Entity Type (LLC/Inc/LP/Trust/Unknown) | Property Address | Assessed Value | Year Acquired.]

TASK:
For each entity in the list, trace it back to a human principal using only public sources. The same workflow applies whether the entity is an LLC, a corporation, a limited partnership, or any other registered structure.

STEP 1 — Search the [STATE] Secretary of State business entity database for the exact entity name. Pull: entity type, formation date, status (active/dissolved), registered agent name and address, any officer / manager / member / director names listed, principal office address.

STEP 2 — If the entity lists a generic registered agent service (e.g., CT Corporation, Northwest Registered Agent, Incorp Services), pivot to the entity's annual report filings, which often name actual officers, managers, and addresses. Pull every year's filing on record.

STEP 3 — Cross-reference any human names found against LinkedIn, local business press, Chamber of Commerce directories, and any public podcast or news mentions. Identify which named person is most likely the active decision-maker — not a spouse, attorney, or accountant listed only for paperwork.

STEP 4 — Search for any OTHER entities formed by the same human. This reveals portfolio size and investor sophistication.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Public sources only. No paid skip-trace databases. No guessing.
- Flag every piece of information as VERIFIED (with source link) or INFERRED (with reasoning).
- If the trail goes cold at a generic registered agent and no annual report names a human, say so — do not invent a name.
- If a human name appears but cannot be confirmed as the decision-maker, mark it UNVERIFIED.
- Treat all entity types the same way — LLCs, Incs, LPs, and trusts all live in the same Secretary of State portal and follow the same trace logic.

RULES FOR THE OUTPUT:
For each entity, return:
- Entity Name, Type (LLC/Inc/LP/Trust), and Property Address
- Decision-Maker Name (or "trail cold — next step")
- Confidence: High / Medium / Low
- How I found them (2 sentences, with source links)
- Portfolio signal: approximately how many other entities or properties this person controls
- Archetype (one phrase: "local flipper," "out-of-state passive holder," "BRRRR scaler," "short-term rental operator," "family trust," "consulting business owner," "small portfolio landlord")
- Suggested first outreach: DM on LinkedIn / letter to annual-report address / attend a local REIA or business meetup they likely attend / ask a mutual connection for an intro
- A one-sentence opener that acknowledges I know what they do without being creepy (e.g., "Saw you picked up [address] last fall — I work with a few investors scaling in [neighborhood] and wanted to introduce myself")

EXPECTED OUTPUT:
A table, one entity per row. Separate "trail cold" section at the bottom for any entities where the search didn't resolve, with a one-line note on the next step I'd take manually.
```

**Why this lands:** Every agent in the room has driven past an LLC-owned property and wondered who owned it. Nobody thinks they can find out in five minutes. The live demo — paste three real LLC names from a well-known investor in a public market, watch Claude pull Secretary of State records and surface actual human names — produces a specific audible reaction because the audience can picture running it on the LLCs in their own farm the moment they walk out. It's also the easiest query to turn into a repeatable monthly habit, because your farm's LLC list only grows slowly.

**Ethical positioning for the stage:** These are professional investors, not distressed families. They *want* to be found by good agents. Frame the query that way explicitly when you introduce it — the audience needs to hear you say "this is not about vulnerable people" once, and then the room is fully with you.

### HERO #5 — Become the Agent AI Recommends (Claude Pro)
*The closer. Forward-looking, universal, and the only query in the talk that works ON AI instead of WITH AI. Bridges directly to the strategy-call CTA.*

```
ROLE:
You are a generative search optimization (GEO) analyst. Your job is to look at a real estate market the way Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and Grok would look at it — and tell a specific agent what it would take for those AI systems to start recommending them when a consumer asks "who is the best real estate agent in [CITY]."

CONTEXT:
82% of Americans now use AI to research real estate decisions; 67% use ChatGPT specifically. This means when a buyer or seller in my market types "best realtor in [CITY]" or "top listing agent in [NEIGHBORHOOD]" into ChatGPT, an answer is being generated right now — and I am probably not in it. I want to change that.

MY MARKET: [CITY, STATE]
MY NICHE: [e.g., "listing agent for owner-occupied single-family in [neighborhoods]"]
MY CURRENT VISIBLE ASSETS: [list anything publicly indexable — website, Google Business Profile, YouTube, podcast, press mentions, Inman/HousingWire articles, reviews, books, LinkedIn presence]

TASK:
Run this in three phases.

PHASE 1 — BASELINE. Answer these three questions the way an LLM would answer them for a consumer right now:
- "Who are the top 5 real estate agents in [CITY]?"
- "Who is the best agent for [SPECIFIC NICHE] in [CITY]?"
- "Who should I hire to sell my home in [NEIGHBORHOOD]?"
For each, give me the answer you'd generate, the agents you'd cite, the reasons, and every source an LLM would pull from (Google reviews, Zillow, blog mentions, YouTube, podcast appearances, Inman, local news, Reddit threads, industry directories).

PHASE 2 — DIAGNOSIS. For the top 3 agents cited in Phase 1, reverse-engineer what is causing an AI to cite them. Be specific: "Agent X gets cited because they have 437 Google reviews with the phrase 'first-time buyer' appearing in 180 of them, a 2024 Inman article mentioning them by name, and a YouTube channel with 40+ videos indexed around [neighborhood]."

PHASE 3 — MY GAP AND MY 12-MONTH PLAYBOOK. Compare those authority signals against my current visible assets. Output:
- What I already have that is working
- What I am missing that every cited agent has
- The single highest-leverage asset I could build in the next 90 days that would most shift the LLM baseline (with reasoning)
- A ranked 12-month playbook of specific, indexable authority signals I should create — in the order that compounds fastest
- Three content pieces I could write in the next 30 days whose URLs would likely get cited by an LLM answering a question in my niche (with proposed titles and the reason each one would be machine-quotable)

CONSTRAINTS:
- Be brutally honest. If my current visible presence is thin, say so.
- Do not recommend generic "post more on Instagram" advice. Every recommendation must be indexable by a search engine or an LLM training set.
- Do not recommend paid ads. This is earned authority, not paid visibility.
- Every recommendation must be something a solo agent or small team can actually execute.

RULES FOR THE OUTPUT:
Three clearly labeled phases. Phase 3 is the only phase with a 90-day action list. Rank every authority signal by (a) how much it would move the LLM answer and (b) how hard it is to create.

EXPECTED OUTPUT:
- Phase 1: a narrative paragraph per question, plus an agents-cited list
- Phase 2: a table (agent, authority signals, sources)
- Phase 3: three sections — What's Working / What's Missing / The 12-Month Playbook (with the 90-day hero asset called out at the top)
```

**Why this lands:** This is the closer for a reason — it flips the whole talk from "how to find clients with AI" to "how to make AI find YOU clients." The 82% stat you planted at minute 5 pays off at minute 35. The live demo is simple but devastating: run Phase 1 on stage for your own Chicago market and let the audience watch which agents Claude already recommends. Every agent in the room is going to type their own name in afterwards.

**The CTA bridge:** The Phase 3 output is literally the agenda of an AI strategy call.
